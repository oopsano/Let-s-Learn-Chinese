数学
shùxué
Math

数 shù numbers

零/〇 líng zero

  1. 一 yī

    2. Unlike other numbers, 一's tone changes if the depending on what follows it in a word or phrase. If it is followed by high (ā), rising (á), or dipping (ǎ) tone, it becomes a falling tone (à). If it is followed by a falling tone (à), then it becomes a rising tone (á)

  2. 二 èr/两 liǎng

    3. While it can be kind of confusing for non-native Chinese speakers, 二 when counting or just using numbers (ex: phone number) and 两 is used with measure words when specifying quantities (ex: two apples; 两个苹果).

  3. 三 sān
  4. 四 sì

    5. Fun fact! Four is considered an unlucky number in Chinese culture because it sounds like the word for death (死 sǐ), similar to how 13 is considered unlucky in the west. Most buildings even skip the 4th floor or avoid rooms with the number, especially hospitals.

  5. 五 wǔ
  6. 六 liù
  7. 七 qī
  8. 八 bā
  9. 九 jiǔ
  10. 十 shí

    11. After ten, all two digit numbers are pretty simple to form. 11 though 19 are just 十 + (number), ex: 十一，十二，十三， 20 - 29 is formed with 二十 + (number), ex: 二十，二十一，二十二，and so forth.

百 bǎi hundred

The hundreds is when the formula slightly changes again. It's important to note that if a number's place has a zero in it (like 103), then you put 零 in that place. You don't skip that place! 一百三 isn't correct, 一百零三 is! Some examples are:

  • 二百零三 203
  • (一)百九十 190
  • 四百五十九 456

千 qiān thousand

The formula again places (number) + 千 infront of the previous sequence, ex:

  • (一)千三百八十四 1,384
  • 五千八百零七 5,807
  • 六千三百九十 6,390
Unlike in casual English, you cannot say "thirteen hundred/十三白", you must say "one thousand three hundred/(一)千三白".

万 wàn ten thousand

You know the deal by now, just add (number) + 万, ex:

  • 三万九千六百 39600
  • 五万零九百七十六 50976
  • 四万九千零三十二49032
Even more so than the previous words, it is important to always use the 万 when using large numbers.

数学符号 shùxué fúhào Math Symbols

  • 加 jiā to add / plus
  • 减 jiǎn to lower / to decrease / to reduce / to subtract / to diminish
  • 除以 chú yǐ divided by
  • 乘法 chéng to multiply
  • 等于 děng yú to equal

分数和百分比 fēnshù hé bǎifēnbǐ Fractions and Percents

While fractions are quite simple, in Chinese the order is reverse. Instead of 1/2 like in English, it's 二分之一 or "two whole's one part" if it were directly translated. An easier equivalent for 1/2 is 一半, but all other fractions follow the (number) + 分之 + (number) format. Further examples would be:

  • 四分之三 = 3/4
  • 十分之六 = 6/10
  • 一百分之三十七 = 37/100
Percentages follow the same pattern, so if you wanted to say "23%", you would say "(一)百分之二十三"

十进制 shíjìnzhì Decimals

Decimals are quite possibly the easiest thing here besides regular numbers. Just add a 点 (diǎn) between the ones and tenths place and you're good to go. Ex:

  • 三十一点五 31.5
  • 十二点九十九 12.99
  • 一百十二点四十五 112.45
